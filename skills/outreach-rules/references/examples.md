# Outreach Examples — All Formats

Annotated examples across all three message modes. Good examples define the quality bar. Bad examples show what to reject and why.

---

# GOOD EXAMPLES

## linkedin_invite

**Example 1** — AI / SaaS / connected tools

> Hi Chris, had a quick thought while looking into your product. When someone searches across multiple connected tools, keeping results accurate while also showing only what they should see can get tricky. I've seen this become a bottleneck. Curious how this shows up in your flow?

Why this works:
- soft entry before the observation ("had a quick thought while looking into…")
- describes a real situation, not a diagnosis
- credibility signal is observational ("I've seen this become a bottleneck")
- one idea, easy to respond to
- no em dashes, no jargon

## linkedin_dm

**Example 2** — Trust and adoption

> Hi Chris,
>
> When people use tools like yours, even when answers are correct, getting them to trust and act on those answers can still be tricky.
>
> I've seen systems where answers were correct, but people still didn't rely on them.
>
> In my own work, I've been trying to focus on making outputs easier to trust, not just accurate.
>
> Curious how this shows up in practice?

Why this works:
- situation-first opening
- credibility line is observational, not a credential drop
- brief personal note adds context without self-promotion
- ends with a simple, open question

**Example 3** — Permissions / connected systems

> Hi Yuhong,
>
> When more tools get connected, even if results are good, making sure each person only sees what they should can become tricky.
>
> I've seen systems where outputs worked well, but controlling what should be shown became harder over time.
>
> In my workflow, I've been separating these steps to avoid that.
>
> Curious if this shows up in your experience?

Why this works:
- situation-first framing
- tension emerges naturally from the situation
- no forced "problem vs real problem" structure
- easy to relate to

## long_reference

**Example 4** — Connected data / trust

> Hi Chris,
>
> When systems like yours start connecting more data, even when answers are right, getting people to trust and act on them can become harder.
>
> I've seen cases where the system works technically, but people still hesitate to rely on it.
>
> In my own work, I've been focusing on making outputs easier to trust and act on, not just improving accuracy.
>
> That shift has changed how I think about building these systems.
>
> Curious how this shows up in your experience?

Why this works:
- layered thinking, still simple language
- no jargon
- builds toward insight
- ends cleanly

# BAD EXAMPLES

## FAIL — Generic, no insight

> Hi Chris, I came across your company and was really impressed. Would love to connect and learn more.

Failure:
- "I came across your company" is banned
- no insight, no situation described
- "would love to connect" is banned
- could be sent to any company

---

## FAIL — Too technical, sounds like interrogation

> Hi Chris, enforcing permissions across connectors seems like a major architectural challenge. How are you solving it?

Failure:
- too technical ("enforcing permissions", "connectors")
- sounds like an interrogation, not a conversation
- no context or credibility before the question
- no humility

---

## FAIL — Abrupt opening, no soft entry (linkedin_invite)

> Hi Chris, feels like as you connect more tools, keeping results accurate becomes tricky.

Failure:
- jumps into analysis immediately with no soft entry
- feels abrupt for a linkedin_invite
- linkedin_invite must start with "had a quick thought…" or similar before the observation

---

## FAIL — Critical tone, no humility

> Hi, I've been studying agentic systems and I believe your architecture may face scaling issues related to permission enforcement and orchestration.

Failure:
- sounds like a critique, not a curiosity
- diagnostic and presumptuous
- no humility, no situation-first framing

---

## FAIL — Credential-dropping instead of observation

> Hi Roop,
>
> The jump from blueprint takeoffs to automated permit compliance is fascinating. I've built AI extraction pipelines that had to handle exactly this kind of structured-but-inconsistent data, and the failure modes are always in the long tail. Curious how you're thinking about regulatory fragmentation.

Failure:
- "I've built AI extraction pipelines" is credential-dropping
- credibility should come from "I've seen…" or "in my workflow…", not "I've built X"
- speaks with too much authority

---

## FAIL — Meeting request CTA

> Hi [Name],
>
> The shift from blueprint reading to automated compliance checking is a massive data challenge.
>
> Would love a 15-min chat to discuss this.

Failure:
- "Would love a 15-min chat" is transactional, not curious
- CTA must be a question that invites response, not a meeting request

---

## Key Patterns

| | Good | Bad |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Real situation described, soft entry for invite | Abstract observation or abrupt diagnosis |
| Credibility | "I've seen…" or "in my workflow…" | "I've built X" or vague "similar systems" |
| Ask | Simple curiosity question | Meeting request or no ask |
| Tone | Exploring, humble | Critical, declarative |
| Em dashes | Never | Common failure pattern |
| Language | Plain, conversational | Jargon, blog-style |
