# Outreach Tone Templates

These templates show the correct structure for each format. Every message follows: **Opening → Credibility → Soft Ask**.

---

## Format: `linkedin_invite` (290–300 characters)

One idea only. No explanation. Fast to read.
Opening MUST use a soft entry before the observation.

### Template

```
Hi [Name], [soft entry phrase]. [Situation in one sentence]. [Credibility signal in one sentence]. [Soft ask]?
```

### Example — AI / SaaS / Automation

```
Hi Chris, had a quick thought while looking into your product. When someone searches across multiple connected tools, keeping results accurate while also showing only what they should see can get tricky. I've seen this become a bottleneck. Curious how this shows up in your flow?
```

### Example — Enterprise / Adoption

```
Hi [Name], was thinking about what you're building. When a system gives correct answers but people still hesitate to act on them, the gap tends to sit in how the output is explained. I've seen this slow adoption down. Does this show up in practice?
```

### Soft entry phrases (use one, then go into situation)
- "had a quick thought while looking into your product"
- "was looking into X and had a question"
- "one thing I was thinking about while looking at what you're building"

## Format: `linkedin_dm` (under 150 words)

Short explanation plus one insight. Can open directly with the situation.

### Template

```
Hi [Name],

[Opening: situation or observation specific to their company — 1-2 sentences]

[Credibility: "I've seen…" or "in my workflow…" — 1 sentence]

[Soft ask]?
```

### Example — AI tool / decision systems

```
Hi [Name],

When more tools get connected, even if results are good, making sure each person only sees what they should can become tricky.

I've seen systems where outputs worked well, but controlling what should be shown became harder over time.

Curious if this shows up in your experience?
```

### Example — Trust and adoption

```
Hi [Name],

When people use tools like yours, even when answers are correct, getting them to trust and act on those answers can still be tricky.

I've seen systems where answers were correct, but people still didn't rely on them.

Curious how this shows up in practice?
```

---

## Format: `long_reference` (under 170 words)

Expand the idea slightly. Show thinking step by step. Still no jargon.

### Template

```
Hi [Name],

[Opening: situation or behavior — 1-2 sentences]

[Credibility: what you've seen or observed — 1-2 sentences]

[One more step in the thinking — optional, only if it adds value]

[Soft ask]?
```

### Example — Connected data / trust

```
Hi [Name],

When systems like yours start connecting more data, even when answers are right, getting people to trust and act on them can become harder.

I've seen cases where the system works technically, but people still hesitate to rely on it.

In my own work, I've been focusing on making outputs easier to trust and act on, not just improving accuracy. That shift has changed how I think about building these systems.

Curious how this shows up in your experience?
```

### Example — AI decisions and explainability

```
Hi [Name],

When AI handles more decisions, people still need to explain those decisions later in real situations.

I've been thinking about this in my own work, not just giving answers, but making sure there's a clear path to understand how the answer was reached.

That seems to matter more in real-world use.

Curious if this shows up in your customer conversations?
```

## Role-Based Tone Adjustments

| Role | Adjust the situation to focus on |
|------|----------------------------------|
| CEO | user behavior, trust, adoption, business impact |
| CTO | system behavior, scaling, tradeoffs (keep language simple) |
| CPO | user experience, friction, workflow problems |
| Head of AI | real-world reliability, what happens in production |

Default → CEO framing if role is unclear.

---

## What Every Message Has in Common

- Describes a real situation — not a claim about their company
- Includes one credibility signal from experience (not credentials)
- Ends with a simple, open question
- Sounds human — no jargon, no em dashes, no technical blog tone
- The founder should feel: "this person actually thought about what we're building"
- NOT: "this person is diagnosing or correcting my business"
